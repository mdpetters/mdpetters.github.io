Course Description

This course is for students with little or no programming experience. It provides students with programming and problem solving skills using MATLAB with emphasis on the systematic development of algorithms and programs. Topics include Boolean math, data representation and types (array, vectors, strings, structures), evaluation of expressions, program control (iteration, functions), algorithm development (pseudo code, physical problem solving, numerical algorithms), basic MATLAB graphics, and limits of computing. Problems and projects are selected from the Geosciencese has no formal prerequisites. However, it is assumed that students are proficient in solving algebraic equations, non-calculus Physics problems, and operating a computer.

Learning Outcomes

  • Demonstrate fluency in the use of procedural statements (assignments, conditional statements, loops, function calls) and arrays.
  • Design, code, test, and debug small MATLAB programs.
  • Create algorithms to solve mathematical problems.
  • Use basic numerical algorithms to process geoscience data.
  • Execute basic vector computations.
  • Demonstrate working familiarity with basic graphics tools.

Course Structure

This course uses short lectures and programming activities. Short lectures are recorded and available through TopHat and/or youtube. The course meetings are via zoom. During course meetings students work in small peer groups on programming assignments.

Curve Policy

All quizzes and exams will be closed book, closed notes. Students who with a documented excuse that are unable to take the test at those times will schedule an alternate time to take the quiz with the instructor. I reserve the right to assign grades using a curve, should the class average be lower than expected.

However, if I were to apply a curve, I would never reduce your grade. If you earned an 95/100 without a curve, you earned at minimum an A.

In case I apply a curve, I take all of the total scores for the class and make a grade distribution. I then compute the average and standard deviation of the distribution. The mean score corresponds to a B- in the course. I then assign the rest of the grades by looking at both the standard deviation around the mean and by looking for natural breaks. The important question is how do I assign A's and D’s and F's? An A grade means someone has demonstrated superior knowledge of the course material. Hence, I only assign an A grade to students who have mastered of course content at the highest level that can reasonably be expected. For students that are on the cusp, I will honor effort. If you come to class, ask questions, are engaged in class, and study the memorization questions you should not receive a D.

Academic Integrity

Students are required to comply with the university policy on academic integrity found in the Code of Student Conduct found at http://policies.ncsu.edu/policy/pol-11-35-01

Students are required to comply with the university policy on academic integrity found in the Code of Student Conduct. Students should refer to theUniversity policy on academic integrity found in the Code of Student Conduct. It is the instructors understanding and expectation that the student’s signature on any test or assignment means that the student neither gave nor received unauthorized aid. Authorized aid on an assignment includes discussing the interpretation of the problem statement, sharing ideas or approaches for solving the problem, and explaining concepts involved in the problem. Any other aid would be unauthorized and a violation of academic integrity policy. Any computer work submitted must be completed on your own personal computer or from your own NC State account to avoid confusion about the origin of the file, and no sharing files in any way is allowed. All cases of academic misconduct will be submitted to the Office of Student Conduct. If you are found guilty of academic misconduct in the course, you will be on academic misconduct probation for the remainder of your years at NC State and may be required to report your violation on future professional school applications.

Cheating is the giving, taking, or presenting of information or material by a student that unethically or fraudulently aids oneself or another person on any work which is to be considered in the determination of a grade or the completion of academic requirements or the enhancement of that student’s record or academic career. Cheating includes, but is not limited, to the following actions:

1. Copying from someone else’s assignment, examination, or other academic exercise;

2. Possessing, buying, selling, removing, receiving, or using, at any time or in any manner not prescribed by the faculty member, any information related to an instrument of academic evaluation;

3. Using materials, equipment, or assistance in connection with an assignment, examination, or other academic exercise which have not been authorized by the faculty member, including but not limited to, notes, calculator, or other technology;

4. Obtaining or attempting to obtain, in a dishonest manner, any material relating to a student’s academic work;

5. Working with another or others in completing an assignment, examination, or other academic exercise when the faculty member has required independent and unaided action;

6. Attempting to influence or change an academic evaluation, grade, or record by unfair means;

7. Permitting another individual to substitute for one’s self in an academic evaluation;

8. Marking or submitting an examination or evaluation material in a manner designed to deceive the grading system;

9. Failing to comply with a specific condition of academic integrity which has been clearly announced in a particular course;

10. Submitting, without prior permission of the faculty member, any work by a student which has at any time been submitted in identical or similar form by that student in fulfillment of any other academic requirement at any institution;

11. Submitting of material in whole or part for academic evaluation that has been prepared by another individual(s);

12. Submitting data which have been altered or contrived in such a way as to be deliberately misleading; or

13. Providing false information to the University in any manner to achieve an unfair advantage, enhance one’s record, or complete a requirement.

Course Schedule

NOTE: The course schedule is subject to change.

Lecture MW 3:00 - 4:50 — Part 1: First Steps, Formatted Input/Output — 08/12/2020 - 08/12/2020

We will learn how to start MATLAB and will familiarize ourselves with its user interface. We will learn how to use MATLAB as a calculator. We will learn about syntax and semantics. We will see ways in which MATLAB provides help. We will learn how to get input from the keyboard, how to print to the Command Window.

Lecture MW 3:00 - 4:50 — Part 2: Relational and Logical Operators — 08/17/2020 - 08/17/2020

We will learn how to use relational operators and logical operators. We will apply relational operators to evaluate if mathematical expressions are true or false. We will apply  logical operators to evaluate if connected expressions are true or false.

Lecture MW 3:00 - 4:50 — Part 3: Scripts — 08/19/2020 - 08/19/2020

MATLAB scripts files are a collection of expressions that consitute a program. We will learn how to create and execute script files to sequentially execute code. We will also learn about the basic behavior of scripts.

Lecture MW 3:00 - 4:50 — Part 4: Conditional Statements — 08/24/2020 - 08/26/2020

Conditional statements are the means by which MATLAB makes decisions about the order in which it executes its statements. We will learn how to use the if-statement, which is the most important method of selection.

Lecture MW 3:00 - 4:50 — Part 5: Loops — 08/31/2020 - 09/02/2020

We will learn how to use the for-loop and the while-loop. We will learn how to construct nested loops.  We will learn to write scripts that contain for-end blocks. We will learn to write scripts that contain while-end blocks. Finally, we will learn to read scripts with  for-end blocks and for-end blocks and predict the outcome.

Lecture MW 3:00 - 4:50 — Part 6: Binary Signals and Code Readability — 09/07/2020 - 09/07/2020

We will learn the definitions of binary, bit, byte and describe advantages of binary communication. We will learn to convert  between decimal lists and characters using the ASCII table. We will learn to evaluate the number of patterns a bit sequence can encode and describe how MATLAB data types logical, char, unit64, and double relate to bit patterns. Finally, we will also learn about how to format code and how to use the MATLAB IDE to automatically format code.

Lecture MW 3:00 - 4:50 — Midterm Exam 1 — 09/14/2020 - 09/14/2020

First Midterm Exam.

Lecture MW 3:00 - 4:50 — Part 7: Arrays and Matrices — 09/09/2020 - 09/16/2020

The basic unit with which we work in MATLAB is the matrix. We solve problems by manipulating matrices, and operators are the primary means by which we manipulate them. We will learn how to define matrices, extract parts of them and combine them to form new matrices. We will learn how to use operators to add, subtract, multiply, and divide matrices, and we will learn that there are several different types of multiplication and division.

Lecture MW 3:00 - 4:50 — Part 8: Functions — 09/21/2020 - 09/28/2020

Functions break up complex problems into smaller, more manageable parts. We will learn how functions let us create reusable software components that can be applied in many different programs. We will learn how the environment inside a function is separated from the outside via a well defined interface through which it communicates with that outside world. We will learn how to define a function to allow input to it when it initiates its execution and output from it when it is done.

Lecture MW 3:00 - 4:50 — Part 9: Algorithms — 09/30/2020 - 10/07/2020

Algorithms are what make code computations useful. We will learn about basic algorithms  such as finding the minimum and maximum value in an array. We will learn how to read and construct pseudocode. We will learn how to represent mathematical series as computer algorithm. Finally, we will learn about convergence criteria to decide when an algorithm is finished.

Lecture MW 3:00 - 4:50 — Part 10: 2D Visualizations — 10/12/2020 - 10/19/2020

Two dimensional graphs tell important stories about data. MATLAB provides excellent tools to create such visualizations. We will learn how to create two dimensional graphs such as timeseries and scatterplots. We will learn about the keyword,value syntax and practice how to style plots.

Lecture MW 3:00 - 4:50 — Midterm Exam 2 — 10/21/2020 - 10/21/2020

Second Midterm Exam.

Lecture MW 3:00 - 4:50 — Part 11: 3D Visualizations — 10/26/2020 - 11/02/2020

Three dimensional visualizations such as contours and heatmaps are common for visualizing geospatial data. We will learn how to visualize 3D functions to relate 3D plots to contour graphs. Finally we will apply contour and heatmaps to setup map projections to visualize data on a sphere.

Lecture MW 3:00 - 4:50 — Part 12: Exploratory Data Analysis — 11/04/2020 - 11/11/2020

MATLAB excels at exploratory data analysis, allowing for rapid plotting and manipulation of tabulated data. We will learn how to read data from Excel files. We will learn how to create histograms and to perform regression analysis. Finally we will apply these skills to analyze weather station data collected at Jordan Hall.
